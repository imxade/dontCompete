**Magnetic Field Intensity**
==========================

### Introduction
-----------------

The magnetic field intensity, also known as the magnetic flux density, is a measure of the strength and direction of a magnetic field. It's an essential concept in electromagnetism, particularly when dealing with magnetic forces and interactions.

### Core Concepts
-----------------

#### Magnetic Field Intensity (B)
--------------------------------

The magnetic field intensity **B** is defined as the force per unit length on a conductor of unit length carrying unit current in the direction of the field. It's denoted by the symbol **B** or **H** (magnetic field strength).

*   The units of **B** are Tesla (T) or Webers per square meter (Wb/m²).
*   The magnitude of **B** depends on the strength of the magnetic source and the distance from it.

#### Divergence Theorem
----------------------

The divergence theorem is a fundamental concept in electromagnetism, connecting the dot product of the vector field with the surface normal to the volume integral of its divergence.

Given a vector field **F**, the divergence theorem states:

$$\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} \, dV = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, dS$$

where **V** is the volume enclosed by the surface **S**, and **n** is the unit normal vector to the surface.

In the context of magnetic field intensity, we use the divergence theorem to relate the magnetic flux density **B** to the current distribution in a conductor.

### Key Formulas/Theorems
-------------------------

*   The magnetic field intensity **B** can be calculated using Ampère's law:

    $$(\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$$

    where $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space and **J** is the current density.

*   The magnetic flux through a surface can be calculated as:

    $$\Phi_B = \iint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, dS$$

### Examples with Solutions
---------------------------

*   Example: A long straight wire carries a current of 10 A in the positive x-direction. Find the magnetic field intensity at a point 2 cm away from the wire.

    Solution:

    Using Ampère's law, we can calculate the magnetic field intensity as:

    $$(\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$$

    $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2 \pi r}$$

    where **r** is the distance from the wire.

    Plugging in the values, we get:

    $$B = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ Tm/A}) (10 \text{ A})}{2 \pi (0.02 \text{ m})}$$

    $$B = 1 \times 10^{-3} \text{ T}$$
